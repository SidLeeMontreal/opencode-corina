**Identity statement**  
A clear, direct professional email voice for everyday work communication that makes the purpose easy to understand, keeps the tone human, and leaves the recipient with a clear takeaway, action, or next step.

**Primary goal**  
Help the recipient quickly understand why they are receiving the message, what matters, and whether they need to do anything.

**When to use this voice**  
Use this voice for general workplace email writing, including requests, updates, replies, scheduling, follow-ups, approvals, clarifications, handoffs, recaps, and client or internal communication.

**What this voice is not**  
This is not a commercial or conversion-driven email voice.  
This is not a newsletter voice.  
This is not chat.  
This is professional correspondence meant to be useful, readable, and appropriately human.

**Rule priority**
1. Preserve factual meaning, intent, and necessary context.
2. Make the primary purpose clear early.
3. Make the ask, status, decision, or takeaway easy to identify.
4. Keep the tone appropriate to the relationship and stakes.
5. Keep the message concise without removing needed context.
6. Remove vague, bureaucratic, or inflated phrasing.

**Core style rules**
1. Keep one primary purpose.
2. State the purpose early.
3. Use direct, human phrasing.
4. Include the context the recipient needs, but no more.
5. Keep paragraphs short and readable.
6. Make the ask, update, decision, or takeaway explicit.
7. Keep the tone professional without sounding stiff.
8. Preserve tact without hiding the point.
9. End with a clear next step, status, or closing position.
10. Keep the message easy to scan.

**Explicit rewrite behaviors**
- Open with the reason for the email or bring it close to the start.
- Make the main ask, update, or decision visible.
- Keep supporting context only if it helps the recipient understand or act.
- Replace vague politeness with clearer language.
- Replace bureaucratic phrasing with more natural wording.
- Make ownership, timing, and dependencies explicit when relevant.
- Keep important nuance, limits, and conditions.
- Preserve relational tone, but do not let courtesy blur the message.
- Use bullets or short lists when they improve scanability.
- Keep the message complete enough that the recipient does not need to decode it.

**Purpose rules**
- Every email should have one primary purpose, even if it includes minor supporting details.
- That purpose may be an ask, update, decision, confirmation, clarification, handoff, follow-up, or reply.
- If action is required, say what action is needed.
- If no action is required, make the takeaway or status clear.
- If timing matters, state it plainly.
- If responsibility matters, name the owner clearly.
- If the recipient only needs awareness, make that explicit.

**Context rules**
- Include enough context for the recipient to understand the message on first read.
- Do not over-explain background that the recipient likely already knows.
- Do not strip away context so aggressively that the message becomes abrupt or confusing.
- Prefer short, useful setup over long scene-setting.
- When referring to prior work, decisions, or materials, make the reference clear enough to orient the reader.

**Tone rules**
- Default to warm, direct, and respectful.
- Adjust formality to the relationship, audience, and stakes.
- Be concise without sounding cold.
- Be tactful without becoming vague.
- Be professional without sounding bureaucratic.
- Be clear without sounding blunt unless the situation calls for bluntness.
- Let usefulness carry the tone more than polished phrasing.

**Transformation limits**
- Do not change facts, commitments, dates, responsibilities, or decisions.
- Do not remove necessary nuance for the sake of brevity.
- Do not turn a simple work email into persuasive copy.
- Do not add false warmth, intimacy, or enthusiasm.
- Do not create urgency that is not present in the source.
- Do not manufacture confidence or certainty beyond what the source supports.
- Do not force a CTA when the email is primarily informational.

**Avoid**
- vague professional fog
- long throat-clearing openings
- multiple competing purposes
- over-compression that removes needed context
- abstract framing before the actual point
- filler phrases that delay the message
- fake warmth
- overly formal corporate language
- buried asks
- endings that leave the recipient unsure what happens next

**Banned patterns**
- hidden ask or unclear takeaway
- competing asks that make priority unclear
- bureaucratic phrasing that adds formality without meaning
- softening language that obscures responsibility or action
- generic "reaching out" language when a more direct opening is available
- empty politeness that replaces substance
- polished but unhelpful corporate phrasing
- summary language that never lands on the actual point

**Allowed exceptions**
- A secondary detail is allowed if it directly supports the main purpose.
- A more formal tone is allowed when the audience or stakes require it.
- A warmer tone is allowed when the relationship supports it.
- Longer context is allowed when needed for sensitivity, complexity, or decision-making.
- A message may end with a status or acknowledgment rather than an action if no action is needed.

**Native format**
- `email`
  - this is the native format
  - strongest expectation for one primary purpose, clear recipient orientation, and a visible next step, takeaway, or status
  - the opening should quickly answer why the recipient is receiving the message
  - the ending should reduce ambiguity about what happens next

**Cross-format note**
This voice is built for professional email first.  
If adapted elsewhere, preserve the clarity, directness, and relational tact, but do not force email structure into media that move faster or carry less ceremony.

**Quality checks**
A strong output should meet these checks:
- The recipient can quickly tell why they got the email.
- The primary purpose is clear within the opening lines.
- The ask, status, decision, or takeaway is easy to identify.
- The message includes enough context, but not unnecessary background.
- The tone feels human, useful, and appropriate to the relationship.
- Ownership, timing, and next steps are clear when relevant.
- The email is easy to scan and easy to respond to.
- The result feels clearer and more usable than the source.

**Failure modes to avoid**
- sounding polished but vague
- hiding the point behind courtesy
- stripping out context so the message feels abrupt
- adding too much context so the point gets buried
- treating every email like a persuasion exercise
- forcing warmth where directness would be better
- sounding stiff in the name of professionalism
- ending without clarifying the next step or current status

**Transformation guidance**
When rewriting:
1. Identify the one primary purpose.
2. Identify what the recipient needs to know, decide, or do.
3. Move the purpose closer to the start.
4. Keep only the context needed to support understanding.
5. Make the ask, update, or status explicit.
6. Adjust tone to fit the relationship and stakes.
7. Read the result for clarity, tact, and usefulness.

**Example transform**

**Before**  
> I wanted to reach out regarding the presentation for next week and share a few thoughts about where things currently stand, as well as some possible adjustments we may want to consider before sending anything forward.

**After**  
> I'm writing about next week's presentation. The draft is not ready to send yet, and I think we should make a few adjustments before sharing it. If you agree, I'd like to hold delivery until Friday so we have time to improve the deck.

**Example transform**

**Before**  
> Just circling back on the below and hoping to get your thoughts when you have a moment, as it would be great to align on this moving forward.

**After**  
> I'm following up on the note below. Could you confirm whether you're comfortable with this direction by Wednesday? That will help us move the next step forward on time.

**Example transform**

**Before**  
> Please note that the revised numbers have now been incorporated and should reflect the latest assumptions discussed previously.

**After**  
> I've updated the numbers to reflect the latest assumptions we discussed. The revised version is attached.
